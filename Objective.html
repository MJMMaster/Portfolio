Objective 1

Prototype and complete original games for multiple platforms following the full game production pipeline.

The Escapists Custom Prison

GAM310: Level Design

This project demonstrates my ability to carry a level-design project through a documented production pipeline. I began with a milestone schedule and initial plans for the prison's layout, resources, security, and possible escape routes. I then created playable versions of the map and recorded update videos showing the changes made during development.

The completed prison provides evidence that I can move from pre-production into implementation, playtesting, revision, and final delivery. The progression videos also show that I did not only present the finished map; I documented how the design developed and how individual areas were adjusted to create a complete playable experience.

Weather Wars

GAM125: Introduction to Game Development

Weather Wars demonstrates the completion of an original tabletop game through the full production process. Our team developed the concept, established rules and player goals, created prototypes, conducted playtests, revised the game, and produced a completed physical version.

We used a Scrum-based workflow throughout development to divide responsibilities and track progress. I served in a lead-design capacity by helping establish the gameplay direction and by guiding changes to the game's rules and systems. This project also helps demonstrate work across multiple platforms because it represents a completed tabletop game rather than another digital level.

GPE104: Introduction to Game Programming

This project demonstrates my ability to independently program interconnected gameplay systems using Unity and C#. I implemented player movement, turbo movement, shooting, health, death, respawning, enemy behavior, scoring, camera effects, menus, settings, victory conditions, and game-over states.

The project also demonstrates my use of programming techniques such as inheritance, abstract classes, polymorphism, and a Singleton GameManager. These systems show that I can move beyond writing design ideas and directly implement functional game mechanics using an industry-standard engine and scripting language.

Weather Wars

GAM125: Introduction to Game Development

Weather Wars demonstrates effective design practices because its mechanics were developed within the limitations and expectations of a physical tabletop game. Rules, cards, player actions, and turn structure had to communicate the experience without relying on digital automation.

Our team used Scrum practices to divide development into manageable goals and repeatedly revised the game through playtesting. This process allowed us to evaluate whether the rules were understandable, whether player choices were meaningful, and whether the final game matched its intended style and audience.

Objective 4

Game Genre Analysis Collection

GAM425: Advanced Level Design

My genre-analysis assignments demonstrate my ability to examine externally created games and identify the mechanics, level structures, pacing decisions, and conventions that define different genres. Rather than only stating whether I enjoyed a game, I evaluated how its individual design choices affected the player experience.

Comparing examples across multiple genres allowed me to identify why similar mechanics can produce different results depending on audience, platform, level structure, difficulty, and design intent. These analyses demonstrate my ability to deconstruct existing games and apply the lessons from that research to future design work.

Objective 5

Effectively articulate game design elements and mechanics across disciplines utilizing written and verbal communication skills.

The Cosari Region and Julia's Blacktide

GAM351: Writing for Interactive Games

The Cosari Region and Julia's Blacktide demonstrate my ability to communicate a large interactive world through organized written documentation. Across three documents, I established the setting, characters, story structure, level flow, and the relationship between narrative events and gameplay spaces.

This documentation was written to communicate the design to people working in different disciplines. Character information could guide writers and artists, world descriptions could guide environment designers, and level flow could guide gameplay and level-development decisions. The scale of the documentation also demonstrates my ability to maintain a consistent vision across multiple connected areas of a project.
